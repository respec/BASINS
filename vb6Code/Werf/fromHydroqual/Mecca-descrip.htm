General Description

Researchers at the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) have developed (Kurt Hess, principal developer) and tested a three-dimensional time-dependent numerical model of coastal circulation called MECCA (Model for Estuarine and Coastal Circulation Assessment).  MECCA was created to study environmental impacts on sensitive marine areas and to assess the use of satellite data for estuarine research.  The model, which is coded in Fortran, was designed to simulate tidal, wind-driven, and density-driven currents in bays and on the shallow continental shelf.

MECCA was originally developed to meet several shortcomings not found in existing models.  It’s features include: (1) extensive documentation, including step-by-step development of the applicable equations and their conversion into finite difference form; (2) creation of a standardized form for the input that requires little explanation, can be adapted to most regional applications, and minimizes model recoding; (3) provision of internal switches to selectively eliminate various terms in the equations for sensitivity testing purposes; and (4) the inclusion of imbedded, one-dimensional flow to better represent rivers and one-dimensional channel flows.

The MECCA model equations consist of two horizontal velocity equations, the hydrostatic approximation, an equation of continuity, an equation of state, and conservation equations for salinity and temperature, and is patterned after two other models in the literature [Blumberg and Mellor (1983) and Sheng (1983)].  However, MECCA differs from those models in that (1) its turbulence parameterization is simpler, (2) it contains switches for eliminating certain terms (e.g., the non-linearities) in the equations, (3) its density equation is simpler, (4) the horizontal diffusivities are constant over depth, and (5) it contains a side friction term for subgrid channels.

Based on users’ comments and recent applications, MECCA was revised and re-released in 1999 in a new version (MECCA2).  With the new version comes additional philosophic goals: (1) to provide a basic, streamlined (less code) version which requires the user to do more external coding, and (2) to read data from similarly-formatted external files to provide time series values for boundary conditions.  For example, the new version reads all data files two records at a time; fewer values are stored at any time, but with linear interpolation the user needs to add more points in time to create a smooth curve –there is no cubic interpolation (which requires four time points).  Other improvements include variable array sizes, corrections to the original code, and several other changes.

Model Methods

The numerical code in MECCA solves the hydrodynamic equations of momentum, mass, salt, and heat conservation using finite differences.  The model is three-dimensional in space, uses a vertical sigma coordinate, has a time-varying free surface, and incorporates non-linear horizontal momentum advection.  The model includes a three-dimensional time variable horizontal diffusion based on the Smagorinsky diffusivity, and vertical turbulent diffusion based on a mixing length and Richardson number-dependent reduction (Munk and Anderson, 1948).

For the horizontal momentum equations, the external gravity wave mode is split from the internal mode.  Variables are placed on an Arakawa C-grid with square cells in the horizontal and at uniform intervals along a sigma-stretched vertical coordinate.  The external-mode momentum equation is solved with an alternating-direction, semi-implicit method in the horizontal.  The salinities, temperatures, and internal-mode velocities are solved with a semi-implicit method in the vertical.  In recent years, some modelers have encountered certain problems with sigma coordinate systems (Haney, 1991).  Many of these problems can be ameliorated by using uniformly-space sigma levels and by subtracting the spatially-averaged density before computing the horizontal gradient;  MECCA has both these features.  The model has the capability to handle imbedded channels that can model rivers in two dimensions (longitudinal and vertical), imbedded barriers to simulate peninsulas or dykes, and triangular cells to better follow the natural coastline.

The model is generally forced with (1) water levels, salinities, and temperatures at the open boundaries representing the continental shelf or ocean, (2) stress at the water-bottom interface, (3) wind stress and heat flux at the air-water interface, and (4) river flow rates and temperatures.

Limitations

MECCA is not designed for deep ocean waters where significant surface and bottom Ekman layers may exist.  The square grid approach cannot always follow natural coastlines or deep channels.  Interacting solutes (e.g., most water quality parameters) are not included.

Applications History

MECCA has been applied to a variety of coastal areas by researchers in NOAA, although primarily to the Chesapeake Bay and its local shelf.  Simulations have focused on tidally- and wind-driven flows, salinity reduction and rebound during high river flow events, sediment transport, and crab larval drift.  MECCA is presently (FY2000) used in NOS’s operation forecast system for Chesapeake Bay water levels.

The model has been requested, distributed, and used numerous times, world-wide.  It has been used for the Gulf of Maine (Brooks, 1992; Brooks and Churchill, 1992; Brooks, 1994) and in Australian coastal water (Galloway et al., 1996; King et al., 1998).  It has been applied to coastal flows in France (Smaoui, 1996; Berthet, 1996).

Model Use Features

The model provides only minimal output features such as gridded fields and time series of selected variables.

Model Support

1.      Users manual – Two user’s guides have been published (see below in references).

2.      Developer Support – The developer, Kurt Hess, provides a modest level of continuing support of the model and applications.  Users requests and questions are answered in a timely manner.  The code is available on request via e-mail from: kurt.hess@noaa.gov, or from a Web site, charmaker.ncd.noaa.gov/csdl/op/mecca.html.
