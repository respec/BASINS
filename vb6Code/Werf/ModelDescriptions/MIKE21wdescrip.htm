General Description

The MIKE system is a professional engineering software package containing a comprehen-sive modelling system for simulation of flows, hydraulics, water quality and sediment transport in streams, rivers, irrigation systems, channels, reservoirs, estuaries, bays, coastal areas and seas.

The MIKE system is a result of years of continuous development and each of the systems is tuned through experience gained from many worldwide applications. Today, the MIKE system is provided with a modern user-friendly interface facilitating the application of the system and a wide range of support software for use in data preparation. Analysis of simulation results and graphical presentation is included.

The model system is divided into 3-software groups; MIKE 11 (the 1-dimensional model), MIKE 21 (the 2-dimensional model) and MIKE 3 (the 3-dimensional model). The 3 model groups all consist of a wide range of different modules which are optional, but where the hydrodynamic module (HD) is the governing module.

MIKE 21 HD: The HD module simulates the water level variations and flows in response to a variety of forcing functions in lakes, estuaries and coastal regions. The system solves the full time dependent non-linear equations of continuity and conservation of momentum. The solution is obtained using an implicit ADI finite difference scheme of second order accuracy.

For MIKE 21 and MIKE 3 different options of the computational grid are available; rectangular grid including dynamically nesting of fine grid areas and curve-linear grid.

When having decided upon the model which addresses the problem in question, a wide range of different modules are available depending upon the nature of the problem: Advection-dispersion module (AD), Particle module (PA), Water quality module (WQ), Eutrophication module (EU), Xenobiotics module (XE) including heavy metals, pesticides, PAH etc., Mud transport module (MT), Sediment transport, Spill analysis module (SA) including oil spill, 5 different wave modules (NSW, EMS, PMS, OSW; BW) and more.

The right combination of modules should provide any engineer or authority with a useful tool in addressing problems and management of:

  • flood control and operations

  • hydropower studies

  • storm drainage analysis,

  • water quality management

  • wave studies

  • point and non-point sources analysis

  • soil erosion and transport studies

  • fate, transport, exposure assessment, and control of pesticides, nutrients and toxic substances

  • risk assessment

  • fate and transport of pathogens

  • etc

All of the models can be set-up in hind-cast, now-cast or forecast depending of the problem and data availability. Also, various degrees of implementing the models can be assessed; from simple set-up using experience and literature as the main data source to very complex set-up implementing e.g. data assimilation.

For more info on the model packages, see: http://www.dhisoftware.com/index.htm.

Model Methods include the following:

This depends of the different modules:

  • 2-dimensional: implicit conservation of momentum and equations continuity.

  • The particle and oil spill modules are based on a Lagrangian approach whereas e.g. the remaining modules are based on an Euler approach.

For the rest of the modules different methods are applied; in the eutrophication module, primary production with different types of plankton and even different algae species are included with the corresponding nutrient cycles, oxygen production and respiration. Also the sediment fluxes of nutrients can be included as well as rooted vegetation. In the water quality module pathogen and different components as ammonia, nitrate and BOD/DO can be in-cluded as both non-cohesive as well as cohesive sediments.

In this small section it will be too comprehensive to include all the different methods involved in the various modules. Reference is made to http://www.dhisoftware.com/index.htm.

Limitations:

All models have limitations. In the modules included in the MIKE series, this is also the case, but the number of modules does not allow for a thorough description of these. Most limitations are due to the fact that in all the modules some assumptions are made and some processes are assumed to be of less importance so they have been excluded in order to save computational time. This also means that with the rapid increase of computer power, fewer limitations are included in the models.

Application History:

At DHI – Water & Environment the MIKE 11 model was the first model to be developed – this started back in the 1960’s. Hereafter came MIKE 21 which is from the 1970’s and MIKE 3 which is from the 1980’s.

All the models have been developed continuously incorporating new techniques and experience gained in research projects and an extensive amount of worldwide applications. Today the models still undergo development – especially due to fast growing computer power. The latter has allowed for significant increase in the amount of computational points (smaller grid size), increase in the amount of components and an increase in the model areas.
